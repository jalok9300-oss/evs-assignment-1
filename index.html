Q.3 DIFFRENNCE BETWEEN EX-SITU CONSERVATION AND IN- SITU CONSERVATION

EX SITU CONSERVATION : Ex-situ conservation is the process of protecting threatened species by preserving their genetic material and individual specimens outside of their natural habitats, such as in botanical gardens, zoos, seed banks, and gene banks. The goal is to maintain populations of species, restore dwindling numbers, and prevent extinction, often through captive breeding and reintroduction programs

IN SITU CONSERVATION ":In-situ conservation is a strategy to protect endangered plant and animal species by conserving their natural habitats. This "on-site" approach focuses on preserving the species within their existing, natural environments rather than moving them to protected, artificial settings. Examples include national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, biosphere reserves, and sacred groves, which protect a species and its entire ecosystem.

Q.5 what is ecological pyramid ?explain different type with example.

An ecological pyramid is a graphical representation that illustrates the relationships between organisms at different trophic levels, showing the flow of energy, the amount of biomass, or the number of individuals in an ecosystem. The base of the pyramid always represents the producers, with successive levels above them representing primary, secondary, and tertiary consumers. There are three main types: pyramid of numbers, pyramid of biomass, and pyramid of energy.

TYPES OF ECOLOGICAL PYRAMID

  1. Pyramid of Numbers

    2. This pyramid shows the number of individual organisms at each successive trophic level. for example: In a grassland ecosystem, there are many individual grass plants (producers), a smaller number of rabbits (primary consumers), and an even smaller number of foxes (secondary consumers), forming a broad base and a narrow apex.

  2. Pyramid of Biomass

    3. This pyramid represents the total organic matter or "living weight" present at each trophic level for example:In a forest, the abundant biomass of trees (producers) is much larger than the biomass of deer (primary consumers) and wolves (secondary consumers).

  3. Pyramid of Energy

    This pyramid illustrates the amount of energy available at each trophic level and the rate at which it flows from one level to the next. Plants capture energy from the sun, but only a fraction (approximately 10%) is transferred to herbivores that eat them, and even less energy is transferred to carnivores
